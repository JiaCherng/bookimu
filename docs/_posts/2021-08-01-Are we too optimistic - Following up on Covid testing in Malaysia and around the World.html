The daily Covid cases in Malaysia has hovered around 20,000 for a few weeks now. At the same time that the situation is deteriorating, the government has laid clear that its last hope was in vaccinating the large majority of the adult population in Malaysia, in the hope that we'll come to achieve herd immunity. Yet all evidence proves that it may not be as easy as some make it to be. Just take a look at the interactive graph below from ourworldindata.org and you'll see that cases are still drastically rising even for countries with world-leading vaccination percentages.

(Source: ourworldindata.org/coronavirus)

Not just that, a depressing upward tick of the curve for Covid cases in Malaysia comes after the government has enforced stricter rules and SOPs, with full MCO (Movement Control Order) in place since 1st June. Even with virtually the whole state of Selangor—which is Malaysia's largest state, thus the state with most cases—being put in Enhanced MCO for 2 weeks since 3 July, the clear upward curve shown above can very well be taken as portents of worse to come. With accumulated cases now being more than 1.4 million, roughly 1.5 in 30 Malaysians have had—or still has—Covid.

And that is not all, from 1881.50 Covid tests per positive case on 5 July 2020, the same figure has dropped to 8.80 on July 27. This drastic drop can very well be attributed to the sharp increase of Covid cases, yet it is another indication that the situation has come to take a turn for the worse. With the blatant exception of Singapore and Australia, Malaysia lags roughly 2 times behind that of most countries and is only slightly better than some of our neighbours in South East Asia. Another fact that needs to be noted is that many Western countries have already employed self-testing kits since the beginning of this year and that most of the results coming from that are not included in the official data while Malaysia has only approved its use on 20 July. This fact clearly dispels the notion and excuse of some who attributed the recent increase of cases to the increased testing. As a matter of fact, it proves that it is urgent that we ramp up testing and inevitable that the number of cases will skyrocket if we do so.

(Source: ourworldindata.org/coronavirus)


When we compare the figure of the first graph with the graph below, the United States and the United Kingdom are 2 countries that stand out. Both with their vaccination rate and their cumulative case. For the case of the United Kingdom, with a leading vaccination rate that led to "Freedom Day" on 19 July 2021, the cumulative confirmed case has shown an exponential increase since.

(Source: ourworldindata.org/coronavirus)


Take this with a significantly decreasing—because of vaccination—yet not negligible case fatality rate, and the only conclusion I can come to is that the outlook is bleak. Malaysia has lost control of its Covid cases since the Hari Raya/Ramadhan celebration. And the current trend strongly indicates that we'll follow the footsteps of the UK—which is definitely not a model for Covid mitigation and control—, albeit a more conservative one. At the same time, the people have to hope dearly that there wouldn't be any more infectious or deadly variants to come. When the time comes as it must, those who had vaccinations will be left to try their luck and hope that they themselves won't be the unfortunate (and currently, few) ones to have severe symptoms or die even with vaccinations.

With an update on 18 August by the Director General of Health, the situation seems to be going in the direction of the scenario I raised above.

Our recent clinical audit data analysis on the “breakthrough deaths” showed that among the overall 12,993 deaths recorded, 80 cases had been partially vaccinated (0.6%) and 4 cases had full vaccination (0.03%).
